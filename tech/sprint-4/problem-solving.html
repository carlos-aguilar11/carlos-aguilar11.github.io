Solving Problems

no always go as you expected


21 April 2023

Getting stuck in a simple problem

I got stuck doing the superFizzbuzz challenge and although it wasn’t an easy task, after spending so much time trying to solve it using different techniques, I left it unfinished, took a break and started doing another challenge. Then I went back, with my last inch of hope and checked my code one last time before I asked for help and I realised that I misspelt the word length inside the function.

Before, that little moment of excitement, I researched the hell out of it on every imaginable website, thinking there was something wrong with my syntax. I also checked the tech channel, used pseudocode to read step-by-step what I was trying to do and checked the error messages, but obviously, nothing worked. Until I decided to have a break, put my mind to something else, went back and ta-da!

I was a bit frustrated that I couldn’t see the problem, found examples that were literally like my solution but: Why is my one not working!? Then once I noticed my “little” - but hugely annoying - mistake the feeling was a mix of relief and satisfaction with feeling like an idiot for not seeing it earlier.

I learnt that sometimes I focus too much in the things that I don’t understand or I’m not used to. For example in this case, I checked everything about my code thinking that something was wrong in my syntax and also I checked the spelling of my variables, parameters and any other element, without thinking that the mistake could be in a simple misspelt of a word that I’m more used to read and type.


Elegantly solving a problem

As part of the JavaScript Kata, we were asked to search for certain methods and functions for each challenge. So in the capitalize challenge, we had to use the “.map” function, which was totally new for me. So, I did what I normally do and search the hell out of it on every single website that Google suggested and I couldn’t find exactly what I was looking for.

We had to map over every word of a sentence inside a string and every piece of information that I found was about using it to change letters from elements but inside an array. ( Do you see those thick words in this paragraph, if you don’t know what they mean, hover over each of them and you can find a link.😉)

After the experience in the superFizzbuzz fiasco, I quickly thought: “Ok, I don’t think this is helping, I’m gonna split the problem, search for each individual thing that I need to do to solve it and translate it to code”. So basically, I used pseudocode, the search engine, reflecting, etc. And it worked on the first try.

This time the feeling was different. I felt pretty awesome that I could sort the solution out so quickly and gave me great pleasure to see my code so clean and simple. This basically was like using my own previous experience to do things in a different way, getting the same result but more efficiently and faster.

My confidence using different problem-solving techniques/processes:


Techniques Scale of confidence Reflections
Pseudocode 9/10 A pretty easy way to start your process, explaining step-by-step what you want to achieve with your coding. I use it a lot, but sometimes I feel it’s a bit messy so I tend to delete it once I reach my goal.
Trying something 10/10 Oh man! In the process of solving a challenge, I use so many different options that sometimes I get them mixed-up. I’m pretty confidente with the technique of trying new things, but I do need to perfect my process.
Rubber ducky method 9/10 This is probably one of the simplest things to do when you want to reflect on what you are doing. I’m pretty confident using it but I only do it when I’m really stuck and I can’t understand what I’m trying to do.
Reading error messages 5/10 I’m not so confident with this one. I can read basic error messages and see where the problem is and most of the time fix the issues, but when the problem is complex or comeback undefined! I can’t work out how to solve it. I guess it’s just the lack of experience.
Console.logging 8/10 This is an easy way to check if your coding is working. I’ve only used it in pretty basic tasks and in that case, I’m super confident. But, I think I have to keep learning about the benefits of this tool in more complex scenarios.
Googling 10/10 This is my way to go. As I mentioned before in my experiences solving problems during the Javascript-Kata challenges, this process is the one I use the most and I’m pretty confident with it. I could say that I’m quite good at “finding stuff”.
Asking your peers for help 9/10 Super confident on this one, but I prefer to ask directly to somebody instead of searching for general help. I feel it’s easier - at least for me - to identify who could help me and ask for it, than just randomly asking and then getting too much information.
Asking coaches for help 10/10 As the one above, super confident with this one. It feels really personalized and it gives me a good way to receive other perspectives from someone with much more experience than me.
Improving your process with reflection 9/10 Reflect it’s a good way to summarise what you learn. I like thinking about what I’ve learned during the day. I also enjoy going back and seeing how I did the tasks I was asked to do. I must say though, sometimes when I check my work - and I guess this happens to almost everybody - I can’t explain, or even worst, I can’t understand how I did what I did to get the final result.

Have I been reluctant to ask for help?

I haven’t, but I do prefer to ask - as I mentioned before - directly somebody who I feel could help me. In general, I’m pretty confident to say something if I don’t understand and asking for help when I need it. I think the only time that I hold my intentions of asking is when I push myself, convincing me that I can do things on my own, but as part of this process of improving our learning techniques, I’ve been pretty good at letting things go when is necessary.
